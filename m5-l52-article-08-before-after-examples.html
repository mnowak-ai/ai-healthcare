What Good Looks Like: Before and After Examples of Healthcare Workplace Communication

Communication skills are built by seeing good examples — and bad ones. This article walks through annotated before-and-after comparisons of emails, memos, and AI-generated drafts, so you can see exactly what to fix and why.

Why Examples Matter More Than Rules

You can read a hundred guidelines about professional communication. But nothing teaches you faster than seeing a bad example side by side with a good one — with specific notes on exactly what was wrong and why the fixed version works better.

Communication errors in healthcare aren't just awkward — they cause missed appointments, medication mix-ups, strained team relationships, and patients who feel dismissed or confused. Every example in this article is based on the kinds of communication failures that actually happen in healthcare settings every day.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Good healthcare communication is specific, appropriately toned for the audience, and actionable. Patient-facing communication must use plain language. Clinical communication must be precise and unambiguous. Team communication must be clear about ownership and next steps.

The most common communication failures: clinical jargon in patient messages, vague action items without owners or deadlines, passive voice that obscures who is responsible, and inappropriate tone for the audience.

AI drafts often carry all four of these failure patterns — clinical tone applied to patient messages, generic action items, passive constructions, and tone-deaf delivery of sensitive information. The human review layer is what catches and fixes these before they reach their audience.
