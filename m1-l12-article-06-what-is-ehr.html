Lesson 1.2 · Current Applications of AI in Healthcare

What Is an EHR and How Does AI Connect to It?

Every AI tool in healthcare either reads from, writes to, or integrates with the Electronic Health Record. Understanding what an EHR is — and what data lives inside it — is the foundation for understanding how healthcare AI actually works in practice.

The EHR: Healthcare's Central Nervous System

An Electronic Health Record (EHR) is a digital system that stores a patient's complete medical information — diagnoses, medications, lab results, imaging reports, clinical notes, allergies, vital signs, and more. Every clinician who cares for a patient reads from and writes to this record.

Before EHRs, all of this information lived on paper — in physical folders, in filing rooms, in the physician's handwriting. EHRs made that information digital, searchable, and shareable. And by making it digital, they created the data that AI now learns from.

Almost every AI tool in healthcare touches the EHR — either pulling data from it, writing results back into it, or using the data it contains to make predictions. If you don't understand the EHR, you can't fully understand healthcare AI.

The EHR isn't just a filing system. It's the record of what happened, the communication tool between care team members, the training dataset for AI, and the legal document that follows a patient for the rest of their life.

The Three Major EHR Systems You'll Encounter

Not all EHRs are the same. Different health systems use different platforms — and each platform has its own approach to AI integration. Here are the three systems that dominate U.S. healthcare:

Epic
~35% of U.S. hospital market
Used by most major academic medical centers and large health systems. Known for comprehensive features and strong patient portal (MyChart). Very popular at systems like Kaiser Permanente, UCSF, and Cleveland Clinic.
AI integration: Epic has integrated ambient documentation, clinical decision support, and predictive analytics directly into the platform. Their AI strategy is called "Epic AI."
Oracle Cerner
~25% of U.S. hospital market
Widely used in community hospitals, government facilities (including the VA and DoD), and international health systems. Oracle acquired Cerner in 2022 and is integrating AI through the Oracle Health platform.
AI integration: Oracle Health AI focuses on predictive analytics, clinical documentation support, and population health management tools.
MEDITECH
~15% of U.S. hospital market
Popular in community hospitals and critical access hospitals (smaller facilities in rural or underserved areas). Known for being more affordable and accessible for smaller systems than Epic or Cerner.
AI integration: MEDITECH Expanse includes AI-powered clinical decision support and documentation features — important for understanding AI in smaller community hospital settings.

How AI Connects to the EHR — Six Key Relationships

📥
AI reads patient data FROM the EHR
Risk prediction tools, early warning systems, and clinical decision support all pull patient data from the EHR to make their assessments. The AI analyzes labs, vitals, notes, and history — all stored in the EHR — to generate predictions.
📤
AI writes outputs INTO the EHR
When ambient documentation AI generates a clinical note, it writes it into the EHR (pending provider review). When an AI tool generates a risk score, that score may appear directly in the patient's chart. These AI-generated entries require human review before they become official.
🔔
AI generates alerts WITHIN the EHR
Clinical decision support alerts — drug interaction warnings, abnormal lab flags, sepsis alerts — appear as pop-ups or notifications within the EHR workflow. These are AI-generated prompts that interrupt clinical staff when the system detects a concern.
🧠
AI was TRAINED on EHR data
The AI models used in healthcare were built by training on historical EHR data from thousands or millions of patients. This is why understanding training data quality matters — the AI learned from real patient records, and any patterns or biases in those records are reflected in the AI's behavior.
🔗
Third-party AI tools INTEGRATE with the EHR
Not all AI tools are built into the EHR itself. Many are third-party applications that connect via APIs (data-sharing protocols). Nuance DAX, for example, is a separate product that integrates with Epic and Cerner to deliver AI-drafted notes into the existing workflow.
📊
EHR data FEEDS population health AI
At the population level, de-identified EHR data is used to identify trends, predict outbreak patterns, and evaluate care quality across patient groups. This is different from individual patient AI — it's AI working at the level of entire patient populations to improve health at scale.

A hospital's EHR generates an automatic alert when a patient's lab results show a critically low potassium level. A nurse receives the alert during a busy shift and acknowledges it without checking the patient's current status. What is the problem?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

The EHR is the digital record of a patient's complete medical history — and virtually every AI tool in healthcare connects to it in some way, reading data from it, writing outputs into it, or using its historical data as training material.

The three major EHR systems — Epic, Oracle Cerner, and MEDITECH — each have their own AI integration strategies. Knowing which system your workplace uses helps you understand which AI tools you'll encounter and how they're built into the workflow.

EHR alerts require human action — not just acknowledgment. Alert fatigue (clicking through alerts without reading them) is one of the most documented patient safety problems in healthcare. Every AI-generated alert in the EHR is a prompt for human evaluation, not a box to check.
