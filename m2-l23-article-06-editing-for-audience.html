Editing for Your Audience: How the Same AI Draft Gets Edited Differently for a Doctor, a Patient, and an Insurance Reviewer

One AI draft, three different audiences, three completely different editing jobs. Understanding what each audience needs — and what your edit must do for them — is how you produce documents that actually work for the people who receive them.

The Same Facts, Edited Three Ways

Here's a situation that happens constantly in healthcare: one clinical event needs to be communicated to multiple audiences. A physician sees a patient with a new hypertension diagnosis. That same encounter needs to generate a clinical note (for the care team), a patient letter (for the patient), and a prior authorization request (for the insurance company).

AI can draft all three — but each draft requires a completely different editing lens. What you add, remove, simplify, or expand depends entirely on who will be reading it. Editing a patient letter the same way you'd edit a clinical note is a common mistake that produces documents that technically contain the right information but fail to serve their reader.

Before you edit a single word, ask: who is this for, and what do they need to walk away knowing or doing?

Editing Rules by Audience — Quick Reference

👤 For Patients
Replace all medical abbreviations with plain language
Add emotional context and reassurance where appropriate
Convert clinical measures to relatable language
Make every action item specific and concrete
Add specific contact information
Include warning signs in plain language
Remove jargon the patient doesn't need to know
🩺 For Clinical Team
Keep clinical abbreviations (they speed reading)
Remove emotional language — facts only
Add specific vitals with timestamps
Add clinical reasoning behind the decision
Include pending items and follow-up actions
Specify responsible parties for each action
Add differential diagnoses considered if relevant
💰 For Insurance
Include ICD-10 diagnosis codes
Include CPT codes for services rendered
Use guideline-specific language (AHA, ACC, JNC)
Document medical necessity explicitly
Show failed prior treatments if applicable
Include provider NPI and credentials
Remove emotional and relational language

What to Remember

One AI draft, three editing jobs: patient letters need plain language, warmth, actionable steps, and contact information. Clinical notes need precision, clinical reasoning, timestamps, and accountability. Insurance documents need codes, guideline citations, and medical necessity language.

The first question before any edit is not "what should I change?" — it's "who is this for?" The answer determines every editing decision: what to add, what to remove, what to simplify, and what to expand.

AI defaults to a clinical register — slightly formal, abbreviation-heavy, and objective. For every non-clinical audience (patients, family members, community partners), the edit must actively transform that register into something the audience can understand and act on.
