Lesson 4.3 · Professional Boundaries

Why AI Cannot Make Clinical Decisions — And What Happens When Healthcare Workers Forget That

The statement "AI shouldn't make clinical decisions" appears on a single slide in the video. It needs a full article. This is why that boundary exists, what it actually means across different healthcare roles, and what has happened in real cases when workers crossed it.

What "Clinical Decision" Actually Means

First, let's be precise. A clinical decision is any judgment that affects a patient's diagnosis, treatment, medication, care plan, or discharge — any determination that requires interpreting a patient's specific clinical situation and applying professional expertise to determine what should happen next.

AI can support clinical decisions in powerful ways — surfacing relevant information, flagging abnormal values, suggesting possibilities, checking for interactions. But the decision itself — the judgment call that a licensed professional is legally and ethically accountable for — must always be made by a human. Not reviewed by a human and rubber-stamped. Actually made.

Five Reasons AI Cannot Own Clinical Judgment

1
AI Cannot Be Held Accountable
When a clinical decision harms a patient, someone is legally responsible. That person is the licensed professional — the physician, nurse, pharmacist, or PA who made or approved the decision. AI is software. It cannot be sued, have its license revoked, face criminal charges, or appear before a medical board. Accountability requires a human. Period.
💡 This isn't a technicality. It's the foundation of why medical licensing exists — to attach human accountability to clinical decisions.
2
AI Matches Patterns — It Doesn't Understand Patients
AI identifies statistical patterns in data. It doesn't know that a patient is exhausted from caregiving, scared of hospitals due to a prior trauma, or that their reported pain level is underrepresented because they come from a community where expressing pain is culturally discouraged. Clinical judgment integrates all of this — AI can't even perceive it.
💡 A physician who knows their patient sees the whole person. AI sees the data points the patient's record happened to capture.
3
AI Cannot Apply Ethical Reasoning
Clinical decisions frequently involve values — not just data. When a patient wants to refuse treatment. When family preferences conflict with patient preferences. When resources are limited and triage judgments must be made. When the medically indicated course of action isn't what the patient is able or willing to follow. These require ethical reasoning that AI cannot perform.
💡 Ethics isn't an optimization problem. It requires weighing incommensurable values — something AI can simulate but cannot genuinely do.
4
AI Doesn't Know What It Doesn't Know
One of the most dangerous characteristics of AI in clinical settings is that it expresses uncertainty and certainty in the same confident tone. An AI that is 95% sure and an AI that is guessing look identical in their output. An experienced clinician develops calibrated uncertainty — knowing when they're confident and when they need to dig deeper. AI doesn't have this.
💡 "The AI said so confidently" is not a clinical rationale. Confidence in AI output is a property of the output format, not a measure of its reliability.
5
AI Recommendations May Not Apply to This Specific Patient
Clinical AI tools are built from population-level patterns. They recommend what worked best, on average, for patients who looked similar in the data. But every patient is an individual. Comorbidities, medications, social circumstances, preferences, and anatomy can all make the population-average recommendation wrong for a specific person — and only a clinician with full context can make that judgment.
💡 "Works on average" is not the same as "right for this patient in front of me right now."

What AI Can and Cannot Do in Clinical Settings

🤖 What AI Can Legitimately Do
Surface relevant patient history the clinician might not have immediately recalled
Flag abnormal lab values or vital sign trends for human review
Check for drug interactions or contraindications
Suggest possible diagnoses for a clinician to consider and evaluate
Predict population-level risks to help prioritize attention
Draft documentation for human review and approval
Summarize a complex record to assist pre-visit preparation
👨‍⚕️ What Must Stay Human
Diagnosing a condition
Ordering treatments or medications
Determining that a symptom does or doesn't require follow-up
Deciding on a care plan or discharge timing
Interpreting ambiguous or conflicting clinical information
Having conversations about prognosis, risk, or treatment options
Making triage decisions when resources are limited

What Happens When This Boundary Gets Crossed

📖 Real Case
IBM Watson for Oncology — When Confident AI Recommendations Were Wrong
IBM's Watson for Oncology was deployed at several major cancer centers internationally, marketed as an AI that could recommend cancer treatments. At some institutions, oncologists treated Watson's recommendations with a level of deference that went beyond appropriate consultation. Internal documents reviewed by STAT News revealed that Watson was producing "unsafe and incorrect" treatment recommendations — recommending treatments that the oncologists who helped build it said they would never use.

At one institution in South Korea, a review of 638 cancer patients found that Watson agreed with the treating oncologist in only 49% of lung cancer cases. At another hospital, Watson recommended chemotherapy for a patient who was actively bleeding — a contraindication a human clinician immediately recognized but the AI did not account for.

IBM discontinued the Watson for Oncology product in 2022. The cases highlight the exact failure mode this article addresses: when healthcare workers defer to AI rather than using it as one input among many, the results can be dangerous. Watson didn't harm patients by itself — it required human deference to do so.

AI tools don't cause harm by existing. They cause harm when healthcare workers treat their outputs as decisions rather than suggestions — and abdicate the professional judgment that licensing, training, and accountability exist to protect.

The Scope Question: What Role Are You In?

The clinical decision boundary isn't just about AI — it maps directly to your professional scope of practice. Understanding what AI can support in your specific role is essential:

✅
Always Appropriate
Using AI to flag information for clinical staff · AI-assisted scheduling · AI documentation drafting (with review) · AI summarization for preparation · Automated reminders and follow-ups
⚠️
Role-Dependent
AI diagnostic suggestions (physicians/NPs/PAs only, as one input) · AI risk scores (licensed clinicians interpreting, not acting automatically) · AI medication checks (pharmacist or prescriber verification required)
🚫
Never Appropriate
Telling a patient their diagnosis based on AI output · Changing a care plan because AI suggested it, without clinician review · Using AI to decide whether a symptom "needs attention" without clinical training · Treating AI output as a final answer rather than a prompt for human judgment

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI cannot make clinical decisions because it cannot be held accountable, cannot understand individual patients, cannot apply ethical reasoning, doesn't know what it doesn't know, and makes population-level recommendations that may not apply to specific patients.

AI can support clinical decision-making powerfully — flagging information, checking interactions, drafting notes, predicting risk. But the actual decision — the judgment call a licensed professional is accountable for — must be made by a human every time.

Scope of practice applies to AI-assisted tasks just as it does to any other task. A front desk coordinator's scope doesn't expand because they have access to an AI tool — and neither does anyone else's.
