SBAR: The Communication Framework Every Healthcare Worker Should Know — And How AI Fits Into It

SBAR is the gold-standard communication framework used in hospitals and clinics worldwide for handoffs, escalations, and urgent communication. Learning it now puts you ahead of most new healthcare workers — and knowing how AI interacts with it makes you even more prepared.

Why SBAR Exists

Healthcare communication failures are one of the leading causes of preventable patient harm. Studies have found that miscommunication during patient handoffs — when one care team hands off responsibility for a patient to another — accounts for a significant percentage of medication errors, missed diagnoses, and adverse events.

SBAR was developed to solve this problem. It gives healthcare workers a consistent structure for communicating critical information — especially in urgent situations where the temptation is to ramble, skip context, or bury the most important point at the end. It originated in the U.S. Navy for nuclear submarines (where communication errors are catastrophic), and was adapted for healthcare by the Institute for Healthcare Improvement in the early 2000s.

Today it's used in hospitals, clinics, emergency departments, and long-term care facilities across the world. If you walk into your first healthcare job already knowing SBAR, you will immediately stand out.

The Four Parts of SBAR

S
Situation
What is happening right now? State the immediate concern clearly and briefly.
B
Background
What is the relevant context? Patient history, current diagnosis, relevant recent events.
A
Assessment
What do you think is going on? Your professional read on the situation.
R
Recommendation
What do you need? What action are you requesting or recommending?

The power of SBAR is its structure. It forces the person communicating to lead with the most critical information (Situation), provide the context needed to understand it (Background), add professional interpretation (Assessment), and make a clear ask (Recommendation). The person receiving the communication doesn't have to hunt for what matters — it's always in the same place.

What AI Can and Can't Do With SBAR

AI tools are increasingly being used to draft SBAR communications — especially in documentation workflows. Here's where AI helps, and where human judgment is irreplaceable:

✅
S — Situation
AI can draft: AI is good at summarizing the immediate clinical fact
If a patient's vitals are in the EHR, AI can generate "Patient presenting with blood pressure 168/102, heart rate 94." The data extraction is accurate. Review for accuracy and context.
✅
B — Background
AI can draft: AI can pull structured history from the record
EHR-integrated AI can surface diagnosis history, medication list, and recent visits efficiently. Still needs review — AI may miss recent verbal discussions or context that isn't in the structured record.
⚠️
A — Assessment
AI should not own: Assessment requires clinical judgment
AI can suggest possible interpretations based on patterns — but Assessment is the clinician's professional read on the situation. An AI's "assessment" is a suggestion to evaluate, not a conclusion to adopt. The human must own this section.
⚠️
R — Recommendation
AI should not own: Recommendations require accountability
AI can surface standard protocol recommendations based on presenting symptoms — but the Recommendation is what you are specifically asking for from another healthcare professional. That ask carries professional accountability that AI cannot hold.

SBAR is a human communication framework. AI can help populate the data — but the judgment and the accountability always belong to the person signing the communication.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

SBAR — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — is the gold-standard clinical communication framework. It structures urgent communication so critical information is never buried and the receiver always knows what action is needed.

AI can help draft the Situation and Background sections — where data from the EHR can be summarized accurately. Assessment and Recommendation require human clinical judgment and professional accountability that AI cannot hold.

Knowing SBAR before your first healthcare job is a genuine advantage. Every hospital uses it, every care team recognizes it, and every supervisor will notice when a new employee already communicates in the structure that reduces errors.
