Why Looking at Real Cases Matters

It's easy to think "I would never do that" when professional boundary violations are described in the abstract. It's harder to feel that certainty when you see the actual thought process — the time pressure, the helpful intent, the convenience that made a problematic choice feel like a practical solution.

Every case in this article involves a real pattern of AI misuse in healthcare. Some are composite examples based on documented violation categories. Others involve published or reported cases. In all of them, the worker believed they were doing something reasonable — right up until they weren't.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Professional boundary violations with AI tools almost never happen out of malicious intent — they happen when convenience, time pressure, and good intentions combine with insufficient understanding of consequences to make a bad choice feel reasonable.

The rationalization patterns are consistent: "it's faster," "no one will know," "the AI usually gets it right," "I'm only trying to help the patient." Recognizing these rationalizations in your own thinking is the most valuable skill this article can develop.

Consequences are real, documented, and often disproportionate to how minor the violation felt at the time. The nurse who entered a name and diagnosis into ChatGPT didn't think that was a big deal. The hospital that had to report a breach and retrain 200 staff members disagreed.
