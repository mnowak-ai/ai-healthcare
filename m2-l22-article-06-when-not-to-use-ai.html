Lesson 2.2 · Using AI to Draft Documentation

When NOT to Use AI for Documentation: The Situations That Require Human Writing From Scratch

AI-assisted drafting is a powerful skill — but knowing when NOT to use it is just as important. Some situations require the full weight of human judgment, empathy, and accountability from the very first word.

Not Every Document Should Start With AI

Learning to use AI for drafting gives you a genuine professional advantage. But the best professionals aren't the ones who use AI for everything — they're the ones who know when AI adds value and when it gets in the way.

AI produces text by averaging over patterns in training data. It can draft a professional memo or a standard clinical note quite well. But some documents aren't about average patterns. They're about this specific person, in this specific moment, and what that moment demands. For those documents, the act of writing from scratch isn't inefficiency — it's the point.

Using AI for a letter to a patient who just received a terminal diagnosis isn't just bad writing practice. It's a failure of professional responsibility.

Six Situations That Require Human Writing From Scratch

💔
Sensitive or Bad-News Patient Communication
A letter telling a patient their biopsy came back positive, their condition is terminal, or their insurance has been denied for a critical procedure requires human empathy that AI cannot reliably provide. These moments define the patient's experience of care.
❌ "Dear Ms. Chen, We are writing to inform you that your pathology results indicate a diagnosis of Stage 3 breast cancer as described above..." — AI-generated language that sounds correct but delivers devastating news with no warmth.
✅ Write this yourself. Take the time. The patient deserves a human response to a human moment.
📋
Clinical Incident Reports and Near-Miss Documentation
An incident report documents what actually happened during an error or near-miss event. It must be factual, precise, and objective — written entirely from your direct observation. AI cannot know what you saw; it will fill in plausible-sounding language that may misrepresent the event.
❌ AI-generated incident report filled with generic language about "the event" and "standard protocols" that doesn't accurately describe what specifically occurred.
✅ Write this yourself, immediately after the event, based only on what you directly observed. Every word must be factual.
⚖️
Documentation Involving Legal or Compliance Matters
Any documentation related to a complaint, grievance, legal inquiry, or compliance investigation carries legal weight. The precision of each word matters. AI doesn't understand the legal context and can introduce ambiguity or language that inadvertently admits liability.
❌ AI-drafted response to a patient complaint that uses language like "we acknowledge that the situation could have been handled better" — an implied admission of fault.
✅ Consult your supervisor, compliance officer, or legal team first. Write carefully with that guidance.
🧠
Complex Clinical Assessments Requiring Judgment
A physician's assessment and plan for a complex, atypical case requires clinical reasoning that AI cannot replicate. AI can structure the note, but the actual medical thinking — why this diagnosis, why this treatment, what was ruled out — must come from the clinician's expertise.
❌ AI-generated assessment that selects the most common explanation for a symptom pattern instead of the correct but less common diagnosis that an experienced physician identified through clinical reasoning.
✅ Use AI to structure the note if helpful, but write the Assessment and Plan section from your own clinical reasoning.
👥
Interpersonal Conflict or Colleague Communication
Messages addressing a workplace conflict, a performance concern, or a difficult interpersonal dynamic require human judgment about tone, relationship, and context. AI will produce something that sounds professionally reasonable but may be exactly wrong for the specific situation.
❌ AI-drafted message to a colleague about a recurring workflow problem that comes across as accusatory when you intended it to be collaborative.
✅ Write this yourself. Consider having a trusted colleague or supervisor review it before sending.
🔏
Any Document Containing Real Patient Information
This is a legal line, not just a best practice. Entering real patient names, DOBs, MRNs, diagnoses, or any Protected Health Information into public AI tools is a HIPAA violation. The document type doesn't matter — the moment you enter PHI into an unapproved tool, you've crossed a federal line.
❌ Copying a patient's name, diagnosis, and treatment into ChatGPT to "help draft a referral letter faster."
✅ Use only approved, HIPAA-compliant AI tools with patient data, or use anonymized/fictional placeholder information when practicing.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Six situations require human writing from scratch: sensitive/bad-news patient communications, clinical incident reports, legal and compliance documents, complex clinical assessments, interpersonal conflict communications, and any document that would require entering real patient PHI into an AI tool.

AI produces text based on average patterns. Some documents need to respond to a specific human moment — a devastating diagnosis, an error that caused harm, a conflict that needs resolution. For those, the act of writing deliberately is not a workaround. It's a professional responsibility.

The question isn't "can AI help with this?" — AI can technically draft anything. The question is "should AI help with this?" Knowing the difference is what separates a thoughtful healthcare professional from someone who uses tools without judgment.
