Gender Bias in Healthcare AI

Women have historically been underdiagnosed, undertreated, and underrepresented in medical research. When AI learns from that history, it carries those patterns forward — often in ways that are invisible until a patient is harmed.

The Research Gap That Became an AI Gap

For most of medical history, the "default patient" in clinical research was male. Women were routinely excluded from drug trials until 1993, when NIH regulations required their inclusion. Before that, medications, dosing guidelines, and diagnostic criteria were developed primarily based on male bodies — then applied to everyone.

The consequences were significant: heart attack symptoms in women present differently than the textbook presentation developed from male research, leading to years of underdiagnosis. Drug dosing that was calibrated for male metabolism caused higher rates of side effects in women. Autoimmune diseases, which affect women at much higher rates, were dismissed as psychosomatic for decades.

When AI is trained on medical data that reflects these historical patterns, it learns the same biases medical practice embedded over generations — and applies them systematically at scale.

The AI didn't create gender bias in healthcare. It learned it. And then it applies it to every patient who encounters it — which means fixing healthcare AI requires confronting healthcare's history.

What This Means for Gender-Diverse Patients

🏳️‍⚧️ Transgender and Non-Binary Patients
AI tools that use binary gender as an input create particular risks for transgender and non-binary patients. A risk algorithm that uses "female" to adjust hormone-based lab values may produce incorrect results for a transgender man on testosterone. Tools must account for actual physiology, not assumed physiology based on assigned gender.
📊 The Data Gap for Gender-Diverse Populations
Clinical AI is trained almost entirely on cisgender patient data. This means tools perform with unknown accuracy for transgender patients — their health profiles, medication regimens, and risk factors simply weren't in most training datasets. Unknown accuracy is itself a safety risk.
🔢 Sex vs. Gender in Clinical Data
Sex (biological) and gender (social/identity) are different variables with different clinical relevance. Many AI tools use them interchangeably, creating confusion and error. Some clinical calculations are sex-based; others are affected by gender-related social factors. Conflating them produces inaccurate outputs for many patients.
💡 For Healthcare Workers
When using AI tools with gender-diverse patients, be aware that the tool's training may not represent them. Clinical observation and patient-reported information may be more reliable than AI scores for these populations. If you notice patterns of inaccurate outputs for gender-diverse patients, that's a quality concern worth reporting.
🧠 Quick Check
A female patient comes to the emergency department with fatigue, jaw pain, and nausea. An AI clinical decision support tool rates her as "low cardiac risk." The AI was trained primarily on male heart attack presentations. What is the most important thing for the healthcare worker to know about this situation?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Healthcare AI inherits gender bias from medical research that historically excluded or underrepresented women. Cardiac risk tools, pain assessment algorithms, and diagnostic AI all show documented gender disparities — most often underestimating risk and severity for women.

Gender-diverse patients face additional risks because AI tools are trained almost entirely on cisgender data. Binary gender inputs, conflation of sex and gender, and lack of representation in training data all contribute to unknown accuracy for transgender and non-binary patients.

For healthcare workers: be aware that AI cardiac, pain, and diagnostic tools may underperform for women. Atypical presentations — especially in cardiac care — may not be flagged by AI trained on male-pattern symptoms. Clinical judgment alongside the AI score is essential.
