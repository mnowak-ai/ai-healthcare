Lesson 2.4 · Patient Summaries

How to Write a Strong Patient Summary: The Key Elements Every Summary Must Include

Knowing that a summary should be accurate isn't enough. You need a concrete structure — a template you can use every time that ensures nothing critical gets left out, regardless of how complex the case is. This article gives you that template.

The Fundamental Rule of Summarizing

Before you write a single word of a patient summary, internalize this principle: a summary is not a shorter version of the record. It is a curated version. Those are two very different things.

Shorter means less information. Curated means the right information — the information the reader needs, in the order they need it, with anything that doesn't serve the reader's decision-making removed. A good summary might be short. But a good summary is always curated.

Cutting information to make a summary shorter is easy. Selecting the right information to make a summary useful is a skill.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A summary is not a shorter version of the record — it's a curated version. The goal is the right information in the right order, not just less information. A summary that omits a critical lab result to save space is not a good summary.

The 6-element template: Chief complaint, Key history (relevant only), Current medications (with doses), Recent test results (flag abnormals), Current care plan, Follow-up needed (with named owner and date). Use this structure every time.

Vague summaries ("has cardiac history," "on several medications") are not summaries — they're placeholders. Every specific that could affect a clinical decision must be included with enough detail to be actionable: stage, dose, value, date, and whether it's abnormal.
