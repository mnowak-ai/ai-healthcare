Small Clinic, Big Hospital: How AI Adoption Looks Different Depending on Where You Work

This certification has highlighted Mayo Clinic and Cleveland Clinic — two of the largest and most AI-advanced health systems in the world. But most healthcare workers don't work there. This article grounds AI in the reality of community clinics, rural hospitals, and smaller practices — where the AI landscape looks very different.

The Reality Check This Course Needs

Major academic health systems have enormous budgets, dedicated AI teams, research partnerships with technology companies, and the scale to generate the data AI needs to train on. They're the early adopters — and they're the ones that make the headlines.

But the U.S. healthcare system is far larger and more diverse than those headlines suggest. Over 60% of healthcare encounters happen in settings with fewer than 10 providers — small practices, community health centers, rural clinics, and federally qualified health centers. Workers in these settings are increasingly encountering AI, but through a very different lens.

Understanding what AI looks like across different settings helps you match your expectations to the reality of wherever you'll work — and helps you advocate for appropriate AI adoption wherever you end up.

The AI tools available to a physician at a major academic medical center and the AI tools available at a rural critical access hospital are very different — but the principles for using them responsibly are exactly the same.

What Stays the Same Regardless of Setting

Even though the AI tools available vary enormously by setting, the principles for using AI responsibly don't change. Whether you're working at a 1,000-bed academic medical center or a 4-provider rural clinic, the same fundamentals apply:

Human review is always requiredDedicated workflows for reviewing AI outputs before actingYou may be the only reviewer — which makes your review even more critical

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

AI adoption varies dramatically across healthcare settings — from highly sophisticated integrated systems at major health systems to basic EHR-included tools at small clinics and rural hospitals. Your AI experience will depend heavily on where you work.

Workers at smaller facilities may have fewer AI tools — but they often have less institutional support for evaluating those tools. This makes individual knowledge of AI principles even more important, not less.

The principles of responsible AI use — human review, privacy protection, appropriate skepticism, and flagging errors — apply equally in every setting. The tools change; the principles don't.
