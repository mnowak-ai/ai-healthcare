Lesson 2.3 · Editing AI Outputs

When to Reject the Entire AI Draft and Start Over

Most AI drafts are worth editing. Some aren't. Knowing the difference — and having the confidence to scrap a draft when the evidence calls for it — is a professional skill that protects you from wasting time editing something that can't be saved.

The Sunk Cost Trap in AI Editing

There's a psychological tendency when you receive an AI draft to feel obligated to work with it — even when it's clearly wrong. The AI did the work. You already have something on the page. Starting over feels like going backward. This is called the sunk cost fallacy, and in AI-assisted healthcare documentation, it can be genuinely dangerous.

If the AI draft would take more time to correct than to rewrite from scratch — or if correcting it would require changing so much that the original provides no useful foundation — reject it. Write a better prompt and start again, or write the document yourself. There's no merit to spending 20 minutes editing a draft you could have written correctly in 10.

The AI draft is a tool, not a commitment. If it's not working, discard it. The professional skill is knowing when to let go.

Five Signals That a Draft Should Be Rejected

These are the situations where editing becomes more work than starting over. When you see these signals, trust them:

🎯
The draft addressed the wrong document type entirely
The AI wrote a clinical note when you needed a patient letter, or a memo when you needed a handoff report. If the structure, format, and register are all wrong, editing your way to the right document takes longer than starting fresh with a better prompt.
❌ You needed a patient appointment reminder. The AI drafted an internal clinical handoff note with SOAP structure and abbreviations.
✅ Reject and reprompt with explicit format: "Write a patient-facing appointment reminder letter in plain language, NOT a clinical note."
🌊
More than half the factual content is wrong or invented
When you do your accuracy check and find that most specifics are wrong — dates, names, medications, procedures — you're not editing a draft, you're replacing it wholesale. At that point, the AI's structure is all that remains, and you can replicate that structure faster than you can verify and correct every error.
❌ You asked for a referral letter. The AI used the wrong patient name, invented a diagnosis you didn't mention, and hallucinated a prior specialist visit that never happened.
✅ Reject. The AI fundamentally misunderstood the source material. Start over with a more explicit, fact-forward prompt.
💔
The document covers a sensitive situation requiring genuine human voice
If you review an AI draft for a difficult patient communication and realize it sounds like a form letter — not because of specific errors, but because the situation demands something more human than any template can provide — that's a signal to write it yourself.
❌ The AI drafted a letter notifying a patient of a terminal diagnosis. The letter is accurate and professional. But it sounds like it came from a printer, not a person who cares.
✅ Write this yourself. Some moments require a human voice and no amount of editing will make a generated letter feel like it was written by someone who genuinely cares.
🔄
The structure or logic is fundamentally wrong for the purpose
Sometimes AI produces a draft that is grammatically correct and well-written but organized in a way that doesn't serve the document's purpose — burying the most important information at the end, putting conclusions before context, or writing in a sequence that makes no clinical sense.
❌ A shift handoff note that spends three paragraphs on stable, routine patients before mentioning the patient in Room 4 who is deteriorating rapidly and needs immediate attention.
✅ Reject or significantly restructure. The most critical information must come first in a handoff note — reorganizing an entirely backwards draft takes longer than writing it correctly.
⚠️
The draft contains language that could create legal liability if uncorrected
Occasionally, AI drafts contain phrasing that would, if sent unchanged, represent an implicit admission of fault, a promise the facility can't keep, or a violation of a privacy or compliance standard. These require either rejection or extremely careful, legally-informed editing.
❌ AI-drafted response to a complaint that says: "We acknowledge that the care provided on June 3rd did not meet the standards our patients deserve." — This is an admission of substandard care in writing.
✅ Reject. Consult your supervisor or compliance officer before sending anything about this situation. Do not edit and send without appropriate review.

When You Reject: How to Get a Better Draft

🔄 The Rejection-to-Reprompt Workflow
1
Identify why the draft failed — wrong document type? Wrong facts? Wrong tone? Too many errors? Knowing the failure mode tells you what the prompt was missing.
2
Rewrite the prompt with the missing elements — if the document type was wrong, add explicit format instructions. If facts were invented, add all key facts directly to the prompt. If the tone was wrong, specify the tone explicitly.
3
Request a regeneration — not an edit of the old draft — don't ask the AI to "fix" the old draft. Start completely fresh with the new prompt. Old drafts create anchoring bias in new generations.
4
If the second draft also fails — write it yourself — some documents, situations, and contexts genuinely aren't good AI use cases. Recognize that and move on without spending more time on a tool that isn't serving you in this instance.
🧠 Quick Check
A care coordinator receives an AI draft for a patient follow-up letter. After reviewing it, she finds: the patient's name is correct, the appointment date is correct, but the AI invented a "prior consultation with cardiology" that never happened, stated the wrong medication (metoprolol instead of the prescribed amlodipine), and wrote that the patient "was advised to restrict all physical activity" when the actual instruction was to gradually increase activity. What should she do?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Five signals to reject an AI draft: wrong document type, more than half the facts are wrong, the situation requires genuine human voice, the structure is fundamentally backwards for the purpose, or the draft contains language with potential legal liability.

When you reject a draft: identify why it failed, rewrite the prompt with the missing elements, and regenerate from scratch — don't try to fix the old draft. If a second draft also fails, write the document yourself. Not all documents are good AI use cases.

Rejecting a draft isn't a failure of the workflow — it's the workflow working correctly. The professional skill is recognizing when editing creates more risk than value, and having the confidence to start over rather than produce something that's almost right.
