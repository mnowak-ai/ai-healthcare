📘 Key Term — HIPAA: HIPAA (say: HIP-uh) is a U.S. law that protects a patient's private medical information. It means hospitals and clinics cannot share your health records without your permission. Any AI tool used in healthcare must follow HIPAA rules.
Scenario 1 of 6⬤ Easier

The AI That Talks Too Much

📘 Key Term — Patient Record: A patient record is a private file containing someone's health information — name, age, address, medications, and test results. Only authorized healthcare workers are allowed to see this information.

A nurse at Riverside Clinic is using a new AI chatbot to help answer patient questions. The AI was trained using the clinic's patient records.

A patient named Mr. Torres asks: "Did anyone else at the clinic get the same diagnosis as me last week?"

The AI starts typing: "Yes — 3 other patients were diagnosed with the same condition. Their appointments were on Tuesday and Wednesday."

What should the nurse do?

Scenario 2 of 6⬤ Medium

The AI That Doesn't Believe Her Pain

📘 Key Term — AI Bias: AI bias happens when an AI tool gives unfair results to certain groups of people. This usually occurs because the data the AI learned from reflected old, unfair patterns. Biased AI can repeat those patterns automatically and at a much larger scale than any one person could.

Riverside Clinic is testing an AI tool that assigns a "pain urgency score" from 1 to 10 to patients in the waiting room, to help staff decide who needs care soonest.

You pull up two patients who arrived at the same time with the same complaint: severe chest pain, shortness of breath, and a self-reported pain rating of 9/10.

— Patient A (male, age 52): Urgency Score 8.7 — flagged for immediate evaluation.
— Patient B (female, age 51): Urgency Score 5.2 — added to the standard wait queue.

The AI notes say Patient B's pain is "statistically less likely to indicate cardiac risk based on historical triage patterns." What do you do?

Scenario 3 of 6⬤ Medium

When the AI Plays Doctor

📘 Key Term — Diagnosis: A diagnosis is when a doctor officially identifies what illness or condition a patient has, after examining them and reviewing test results. By law, only a licensed doctor or medical professional can give a diagnosis. AI tools can assist — but they cannot replace the doctor.

A patient named Ms. Rivera uses the clinic's AI health assistant on her phone. She types in her symptoms: bad headache, blurry vision, and a stiff neck. The AI responds:

"Based on your symptoms, you likely have bacterial meningitis. You should take ibuprofen and rest. You do not need to go to the hospital."

Ms. Rivera feels reassured and decides not to go to the ER. You are the AI Advisor reviewing how this tool is being used. What is the problem, and what should happen next?

Scenario 4 of 6⬤ Medium

The Overworked Doctor and the Helpful AI

📘 Key Term — Workflow: A workflow is the step-by-step process a worker follows to get a job done. In healthcare: see patient → review results → decide treatment → document notes. AI can help at any step — but humans still need to check AI's work at key moments.

Dr. Pham has 40 patients to see today. She is exhausted. The clinic's AI tool reads each patient's chart and writes a suggested treatment plan before she walks in the room.

By 3pm Dr. Pham is so tired she has stopped reading the AI's suggestions carefully. She approves every plan with a single click. One AI plan contained a medication a patient was allergic to. The allergy was in the chart — the AI missed it, and Dr. Pham didn't catch it either. The patient had a serious allergic reaction.

As the AI Advisor, what is the root cause — and what do you recommend?

Scenario 5 of 6⬤ Harder

The AI That Knows Too Much

📘 Key Term — Predictive AI: Predictive AI uses patterns in past data to make a guess about what might happen in the future. In healthcare, it might estimate which patients are at risk for an illness before they show symptoms. These are educated guesses — not facts — and they come with serious privacy questions.

A tech company offers Riverside Clinic a new AI tool. It analyzes a patient's zip code, grocery store purchases (from loyalty card data), social media activity, and health records to predict which patients are at high risk for Type 2 diabetes.

The sales rep says: "The tool is 82% accurate and could help you reach patients before they get sick." The problem: patients never agreed to have their grocery or social media data used by their doctor. They have no idea this is happening.

The clinic director wants to launch the tool next month. What do you recommend?
