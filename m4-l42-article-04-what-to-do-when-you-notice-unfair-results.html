Lesson 4.2 · AI Bias & Equity

What Should You Do When You Notice AI Giving Unfair Results?

Knowing that AI bias exists is one thing. Knowing exactly what to do when you see it in front of you — in a real shift, with a real patient — is what this article is about. This is the practical playbook every healthcare worker needs.

The Moment You Notice Something Seems Off

You're reviewing an AI output and something doesn't sit right. The risk score seems too low for a patient who looks quite unwell. The recommendation seems different from what you'd expect for a patient with this presentation. Or you've noticed over the past few weeks that patients of a particular background consistently seem to get lower scores from this tool.

What do you do? This is the moment that matters — and most healthcare workers haven't been trained for it. The gap between "noticing something" and "doing something about it" is where AI bias continues unchecked. Closing that gap is one of the most important things you can do as a healthcare worker in the AI era.

You don't need to be a data scientist to act on AI bias. You need to trust what you're observing, document it clearly, and know where to take it. The rest is someone else's job — your job is to make sure it gets there.

A Five-Step Response Framework

1
Trust your observation — don't dismiss it
The first instinct many workers have is to assume they're wrong and the AI is right. Resist this. AI tools have documented biases. If something seems systematically off, it may be. Your clinical observation of real patients is valuable evidence that the algorithm has never seen.
💡 The difference between a single outlier and a pattern: if you notice the discrepancy once, note it. If you see it two or three times with similar patient profiles, that's a pattern worth acting on.
2
Protect the patient in front of you right now
Before anything else, make sure the patient in front of you gets appropriate care. If an AI score seems too low for a patient who appears unwell, document your clinical assessment, communicate it to the responsible clinician, and ensure it's noted in the record. The system-level problem needs to be fixed — but the patient's immediate needs come first.
💡 Always ask: does acting on this AI output serve this patient's best interest, given what I'm observing clinically? If there's a gap, your clinical judgment takes precedence.
3
Document what you observed — specifically
Vague concerns don't move organizations. Specific, documented observations do. Write down the patient scenario, the AI output that seemed problematic, and what clinical reality seemed to differ from it. If it's a pattern, document multiple examples. Dates, patient identifiers (in a HIPAA-compliant way), and specific discrepancies make a much stronger case than a general concern.
💡 You don't need to prove the AI is biased — you just need to describe what you observed. That's enough to trigger an investigation by the people with the tools to assess it properly.
4
Report through the right channel
Most healthcare organizations have established channels for quality improvement concerns — a supervisor, a quality improvement team, a patient safety hotline, or a compliance officer. AI bias concerns fit into quality improvement. You don't need a special "AI problem" reporting form — you need to get your documented observation to a person who has the authority to investigate.
💡 If you're unsure who to tell, start with your direct supervisor. Frame it as a quality observation: "I've noticed a pattern in our [tool name] outputs that I'd like to flag for quality review."
5
Follow up — and protect yourself
After reporting, keep a personal record of what you reported, when, and to whom. If the concern isn't addressed in a reasonable timeframe, it's appropriate to escalate further. In rare cases where an AI tool seems to be causing active patient harm and nothing is being done, you may have professional or legal obligations to continue escalating.
💡 Reporting a legitimate quality concern is a protected professional activity. You should not face retaliation for raising a documented, good-faith observation about an AI tool's performance.

Common Barriers — and How to Get Past Them

😰 "What if I'm wrong?"
You're not claiming the AI is definitely biased — you're reporting an observation that warrants investigation. You can't be "wrong" about what you observed.
💡 Frame it as: "I've noticed a pattern I'd like flagged for quality review" — not "this tool is biased."
😶 "I don't want to cause trouble"
Reporting a quality concern through proper channels is not causing trouble — it's doing your job. AI quality issues affect patient safety.
💡 Patient safety reporting is protected activity. Your organization wants to know about these issues.
🤷 "I don't know who to tell"
Start with your direct supervisor. If they don't know who to route it to, ask about quality improvement or patient safety reporting channels.
💡 Most organizations have a quality improvement process that handles exactly this type of observation.
⏰ "I don't have time"
A one-paragraph written observation takes five minutes. The documentation doesn't need to be comprehensive — it needs to be specific enough to investigate.
💡 Write it down during your break or at the end of shift: date, tool name, patients involved (HIPAA-compliant), what you noticed.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

When you notice AI giving seemingly unfair results: trust your observation, protect the patient in front of you immediately, document the specific examples, report through proper channels, and follow up to ensure the concern is being addressed.

You don't need to prove bias to report it. You need to describe what you observed clearly and specifically. The investigation is someone else's responsibility — your responsibility is to make sure the concern reaches the right people.

"Vendor validation" and "the tool is approved" are not responses to a specific pattern of concerning outputs. If a first report doesn't get traction, escalating with specific documented evidence is appropriate and professionally protected.
