Lesson 2.3 · Editing AI Outputs

Accuracy First: Why Healthcare AI Gets Facts Wrong and How to Catch Every Error

AI produces factual errors with total confidence. In healthcare, those errors don't just reflect badly on the writer — they can redirect care, delay treatment, or cause direct patient harm. This article teaches you exactly what to look for and how to find it every time.

Why AI Gets Facts Wrong in Healthcare

AI doesn't look things up. It predicts what text should come next based on patterns from its training data. This means when it writes "the patient takes metoprolol 50mg twice daily," it isn't pulling that from a medical record — it's generating text that fits the pattern of how clinical notes about metoprolol tend to be written.

If you gave the AI incorrect information in your prompt, it will repeat it confidently. If the AI doesn't have a specific detail, it will often fill in something plausible-sounding. The result is a document that reads like a reliable clinical record but may contain invented, misremembered, or pattern-matched facts in place of real ones.

AI doesn't distinguish between facts it knows and facts it invented. Both read exactly the same way. That's what makes accuracy checking non-negotiable.

The 6 Types of Factual Errors AI Commonly Makes

These are the categories of errors that appear most often in AI-generated healthcare documents. Knowing the types helps you look for them systematically:

📅
Date and Time Errors
AI frequently generates impossible dates, transposes month and day, or calculates relative dates incorrectly ("3 weeks after June 10" might come back as July 10 instead of July 1).
❌ AI generated
"Follow-up on February 30th"
✅ Correct
"Follow-up on March 2nd"
💊
Medication Errors
AI can confuse drug names (lisinopril vs. losartan), invent dosages, change routes of administration, or use a brand name where a generic was intended — or vice versa.
❌ AI generated
"Metformin 2000mg once daily"
✅ Correct
"Metformin 1000mg twice daily"
👤
Name and Identity Errors
AI may substitute similar-sounding names, switch patient and provider names, use the wrong pronoun, or carry a name from a previous context into the current document.
❌ AI generated
"Dr. Martinez reviewed the chart and the patient, Dr. Chen, was notified"
✅ Correct
"Dr. Martinez reviewed the chart. Patient Rosa Chen was notified."
🔢
Numerical Errors
AI may transpose digits, round numbers incorrectly, confuse units (mg vs. mcg, mL vs. L), or generate plausible-looking numbers that don't match the actual data provided.
❌ AI generated
"BP 185/94 → normalized to 128/82 after treatment" (actual was 158/94)
✅ Correct
"BP 158/94 → normalized to 128/82 after treatment"
🏥
Invented Procedures or Events
AI sometimes adds procedures, consultations, or events that were never mentioned in the prompt — filling gaps with clinically plausible but invented information.
❌ AI generated
"Patient was evaluated by cardiology prior to discharge" (no cardiology consult was requested)
✅ Correct
"Patient was not evaluated by cardiology. Outpatient cardiology referral placed."
🔄
Concept Confusion
AI sometimes confuses two related but distinct clinical concepts — like hypertension and hypotension, type 1 and type 2 diabetes, or systolic and diastolic blood pressure values.
❌ AI generated
"Patient has a history of hyperthyroidism, currently on levothyroxine" (levothyroxine treats HYPOthyroidism)
✅ Correct
"Patient has a history of hypothyroidism, currently on levothyroxine"

A Systematic Method for Catching Every Error

Knowing what types of errors exist isn't enough — you need a method for finding them reliably. This four-step accuracy verification process works for any healthcare document:

🔬 Accuracy Verification Method
Four Steps to Find Every Factual Error
Use these steps in sequence. Each targets a different category of error.
1

Highlight every specific — before you evaluate anything

Go through the document and mentally highlight (or literally underline) every specific piece of information: every name, every date, every number, every medication, every procedure. This forces you to see each specific as a claim that needs verification, not just part of a sentence you're reading for flow.

📌 Ask: "How many verifiable facts are in this document?" Then check each one.
2

Cross-check each specific against your source

For each highlighted specific, go back to the original source — the patient record, the original notes, the information you were given — and verify the match. Don't rely on memory. The most dangerous errors are the ones that are almost right.

⚠️ "Almost right" is worse than obviously wrong. A wrong medication dose looks identical to a right one until someone checks it.
3

Flag anything AI added that you didn't provide

Compare the document's content against what you put in your prompt. Did the AI add any information you didn't provide? Any event you didn't mention? Any detail that sounds plausible but you can't trace back to your source? Flag those for removal or verification.

📌 The rule: If you can't trace it to your source, don't keep it. AI-invented information that sounds correct is more dangerous than an obvious error.
4

Do a concept check on clinical terms

For any clinical concepts in the document, verify that the terms make medical sense together. Does the medication match the diagnosis? Does the recommended action match the condition described? Does the document contain any pairs of opposite terms where one might have been confused for the other?

📌 Classic confusion pairs to watch for: hyper/hypo · systolic/diastolic · type 1/type 2 · mg/mcg · QD/QOD · bilateral/unilateral

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Six types of AI factual errors: date/time errors, medication errors, name/identity errors, numerical errors, invented procedures or events, and concept confusion. Each appears with equal confidence in AI-generated text — the only way to catch them is systematic verification, not intuition.

The four-step accuracy method: (1) Highlight every specific before evaluating anything. (2) Cross-check each specific against your source — don't rely on memory. (3) Flag anything AI added that wasn't in your prompt. (4) Do a concept check on clinical term pairs.

"Almost right" is more dangerous than obviously wrong — because almost-right errors pass the casual eye test. A wrong medication that looks right, a date that's almost correct, a dosage that's close but not accurate: these are the errors that cause harm precisely because they don't trigger alarm.
