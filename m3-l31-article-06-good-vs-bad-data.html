What Good Data Looks Like vs. Bad Data: How Data Quality Affects AI Recommendations

AI is only as reliable as the data it's working from. Understanding what makes healthcare operational data "good" or "bad" — and what bad data does to AI recommendations — is one of the most important practical skills in this module.

The Fundamental Rule: Garbage In, Garbage Out

In computer science, there's a phrase that's been true since the 1950s and is still true today: "garbage in, garbage out." It means that if you feed bad data into any system — AI or otherwise — you will get bad results out of it. The system itself might be perfectly built. The algorithm might be state-of-the-art. But it cannot compensate for the quality of what you give it.

In healthcare operations, this matters enormously. AI scheduling tools, staffing systems, and inventory managers all rely on operational data that is accurate, current, and complete. When that data is wrong, the AI's recommendations are wrong — sometimes dangerously so. And because AI presents its outputs confidently (it doesn't say "I'm not sure about this"), wrong recommendations can look just as trustworthy as correct ones.

This is why data quality is everyone's responsibility — not just the IT team's. Every time a healthcare worker logs an appointment, records a supply count, or updates a patient status, they're contributing to the data that AI systems will use to make recommendations. The quality of their inputs affects the quality of everyone's outputs.

The Four Dimensions of Data Quality

When data scientists and healthcare IT professionals talk about "data quality," they mean four specific things. Understanding all four helps you recognize when data might be unreliable — before you act on an AI recommendation built from it.

✅
Accuracy
Does the data correctly reflect what actually happened?
✅ Good: Appointment logged as "no-show" when patient genuinely didn't arrive
❌ Bad: Appointment logged as "no-show" because the staff forgot to check in the patient who was actually there
🕒
Timeliness
Is the data current — updated when things actually change?
✅ Good: Supply counts updated in real time as items are used or received
❌ Bad: Supply counts updated manually at the end of each day — AI thinks you have 40 gloves at 2pm when you actually ran out at 11am
📋
Completeness
Is all the necessary information present, with no critical gaps?
✅ Good: Every appointment record includes date, time, provider, patient ID, appointment type, and outcome (attended/no-show/cancelled)
❌ Bad: 30% of appointment records are missing the "appointment type" field — AI can't accurately predict appointment duration or staffing needs
🔄
Consistency
Is data entered the same way across different people and systems?
✅ Good: All staff use the same codes — "NS" for no-show, "CAN" for cancellation, "LAT" for late arrival
❌ Bad: Some staff enter "no show," others "NS," others "no-show," others "patient absent" — the AI treats these as four different categories and can't accurately count no-shows

What to Remember From This Article

Garbage in, garbage out. AI cannot compensate for poor data quality — and it presents flawed outputs with the same confidence as reliable ones. Data quality is the invisible foundation that determines whether AI recommendations can be trusted.

The four dimensions of data quality: Accuracy (does it reflect reality?), Timeliness (is it current?), Completeness (are there gaps?), and Consistency (is it entered the same way every time?). All four matter.

Data quality is everyone's responsibility. Every time you log an appointment outcome, update a supply count, or record a patient status, you're contributing to the data that AI systems will use. The accuracy of your inputs affects the reliability of everyone's recommendations.

When an AI recommendation surprises you or contradicts your experience, data quality problems are often the explanation. A recommendation that seems wrong is a signal to investigate the data behind it — not just to override it and move on.
