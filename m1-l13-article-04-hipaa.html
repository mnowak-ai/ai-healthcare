Lesson 1.3 · AI Limitations in Healthcare

Privacy, HIPAA, and AI: What Every Healthcare Worker Must Know Before Using These Tools

HIPAA has existed since 1996. AI has arrived in healthcare in earnest over the last decade. The interaction between the two creates real questions — and real risks — that every healthcare worker who touches a patient record or uses an AI tool must understand. This article makes it practical and clear.

HIPAA in 60 Seconds

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) is the federal law that governs how patient health information is collected, stored, and shared. It applies to healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses — and their business partners.

The core principle is simple: patient health information belongs to the patient — not the hospital, not the app, not the AI company. Healthcare workers and organizations can use it only for specific permitted purposes, must protect it from unauthorized access, and must have documented agreements with any external entities that handle it.

🔒
Protected Health Information (PHI)
Any information that can identify a patient and relates to their health, treatment, or payment. Includes names, dates, medical record numbers, diagnoses, lab results, medications, and more.
📄
Business Associate Agreement (BAA)
A legal contract required between a healthcare organization and any outside vendor that handles PHI. AI vendors who process patient data must have a BAA — without one, using their tools with real patient data is a HIPAA violation.
⚖️
Minimum Necessary Standard
Healthcare workers should only access and share the minimum amount of patient information necessary to accomplish the task at hand — even when using AI tools that might have access to the full record.

HIPAA doesn't say you can't use AI with patient data. It says you must have the right agreements, protections, and practices in place when you do. The "right tool" question is also always a "right compliance" question.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

HIPAA protects patient health information and applies to any tool or vendor that handles it — including AI tools. Using an AI tool with real patient data requires a Business Associate Agreement between the vendor and your organization.

The most common AI-related HIPAA violations come from well-meaning workers using unauthorized tools — free AI chatbots, personal devices, consumer apps — with real patient data. The intent doesn't matter; the compliance breach does.

Before using any AI tool with patient information: ask whether your organization has a BAA with that vendor. If the answer is no — or "I don't know" — use your organization's approved tools instead and flag the question to your supervisor.
