AI Writing Tools in Healthcare: A Field Guide to the Tools You'll Actually Encounter

Healthcare workers increasingly encounter AI documentation tools on the job — often with minimal training. This guide gives you a clear overview of the major tools in the field, what they do, and what you need to know before using each one.

Why You Need to Know the Landscape

Walking into a new healthcare job and encountering an AI documentation tool for the first time without any context is increasingly common — and uncomfortable. You might not know what the tool does, whether it's approved for certain types of documentation, or what your review responsibilities are.

This field guide gives you a baseline so you're never starting from zero. You won't be an expert in every tool, but you'll understand the categories, know the key questions to ask, and have a framework for using any AI documentation tool responsibly.

You don't need to know every AI tool in healthcare. You need to know how to think about any AI tool — what it does, what it can't do, and what review it requires from you.

What to Remember

Healthcare AI documentation tools fall into four main categories: clinical documentation AI (trained on medical data), ambient AI (listens and auto-drafts), administrative AI (scheduling, billing, communications), and general AI tools (not healthcare-specific). Each has different capabilities and different review requirements.

Before using any AI documentation tool at work, ask four questions: Is it approved for this use case and user role? Has it been reviewed for HIPAA compliance? What are the review requirements? Who is accountable for the output?

General AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) can be useful for healthcare writing tasks but must never receive real patient information. They are not HIPAA-compliant, not trained on clinical data, and not approved for clinical documentation. Use them for learning, drafting templates, and non-PHI tasks only.
