Can I Use ChatGPT for Work? A Healthcare Worker's Guide to AI Tools and Patient Privacy

This is the question every healthcare worker is already asking — and most haven't gotten a clear answer to. The honest answer is: sometimes yes, often no, and the difference matters enormously for your career.

The Question Everyone Is Asking

AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and dozens of others are genuinely useful. They can draft emails, summarize information, answer questions, and help organize ideas in seconds. Healthcare workers see this and naturally wonder: can I use these tools to make my work faster and better?

The answer is not a simple yes or no. It depends entirely on what information you're entering into the tool, whether the tool is approved by your employer, and whether a proper compliance agreement is in place.

🚨 Critical Rule
Consumer AI Tools Are Not HIPAA-Compliant By Default
The free or standard versions of ChatGPT, Claude, Google Gemini, and most other general-purpose AI tools have NOT signed a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with your healthcare organization. This means they are not authorized to receive, process, or store Protected Health Information — regardless of your intentions, regardless of your training, and regardless of whether you think the data is "basically harmless." Entering patient information into these tools is a HIPAA violation.

What Makes an AI Tool HIPAA-Compliant?

HIPAA compliance for an AI tool isn't automatic — it requires specific steps. Here's what separates a compliant tool from a risky one:

The key document is the Business Associate Agreement (BAA). This is a legal contract that makes a vendor legally responsible for protecting PHI they receive. Without a BAA, no third-party tool can legally receive patient data — and most consumer AI tools have not signed one with your organization.

The Three Questions to Ask Before Using Any AI Tool

Before entering anything into an AI tool at work, run it through these three questions. If any answer is "no" or "I don't know," stop and check with your supervisor or IT department first:

1
Is this tool approved by my employer for healthcare use?
Your organization's IT or compliance team maintains a list of approved tools. If it's not on the list, don't use it with any patient-related information. "I didn't know it wasn't approved" doesn't protect you from consequences.
Rule: Default to "no" until you verify "yes"
2
Does the information I'm entering include any PHI?
Review the 18 PHI identifiers from the previous article. Names, dates, medical record numbers, diagnoses, zip codes combined with other data — any of these in combination with health information creates PHI. When in doubt, assume it's PHI.
Rule: If it could identify a patient, treat it as PHI
3
Has a Business Associate Agreement been signed between my employer and this tool's vendor?
You may not know this answer yourself — and that's fine. If you can't confirm it, ask your supervisor or IT department before proceeding. The BAA is what legally authorizes the tool to receive PHI.
Rule: No BAA = no PHI in that tool, ever

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Consumer AI tools (free ChatGPT, Google Gemini, etc.) are not HIPAA-compliant by default. They have not signed a Business Associate Agreement with your organization, which means entering any PHI into them is a HIPAA violation — full stop.

Before using any AI tool with work-related content, ask three questions: Is it employer-approved? Does my input include PHI? Has a BAA been signed? If any answer is "no" or "I don't know," check first.

There are many things you can use general AI tools for at work — drafting general templates, researching medical topics without patient data, creating staff training content. The line is not "AI tools at work = always wrong." The line is "PHI in non-approved AI tools = always wrong."
