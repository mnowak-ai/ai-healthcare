Lesson 4.3 · Professional Boundaries

The Three Questions Every Healthcare Worker Should Ask Before Using AI on Any Task

The video gives you a one-slide framework: Is it appropriate? Is it allowed? Is it safe? This article turns those three questions into a real decision-making tool — with edge cases, examples, and five complete scenario walkthroughs you can apply to anything you encounter on the job.

Why You Need a Framework, Not Just Rules

Healthcare AI is evolving faster than workplace policies can keep up with. New tools appear constantly. Approved tools get new features. Colleagues recommend workarounds. Vendors promise capabilities that haven't been fully evaluated. In this environment, waiting for a specific rule that covers every situation you'll encounter isn't a viable strategy.

What you need is a framework — a set of questions you can apply to any new situation to make a sound professional judgment, even when no one has written the specific rule yet. These three questions work for every AI tool, every task, and every patient context. Master them and you'll rarely find yourself in a situation you can't navigate confidently.

The goal isn't to memorize every AI policy your organization has ever written. It's to develop the judgment to make the right call when you encounter a situation no policy anticipated — which will happen regularly.

The Three Questions — In Depth

1
Question One
Is it appropriate? Would a reasonable, ethical healthcare professional be comfortable with this use?
This is the values question. Before you even think about rules, ask whether the use itself makes sense — whether it serves the patient well, honors their dignity and privacy, and reflects the kind of professional you want to be. A lot of AI misuse happens in situations that are technically allowed but ethically off.
✅ Likely Appropriate
Using AI to draft a patient follow-up letter that a clinician will review and personalize before sending
🚫 Not Appropriate
Using AI to generate a grief counseling response for a patient who just received a terminal diagnosis — even if the output is technically accurate
💡 The appropriateness test: "Would I be comfortable if this patient, my supervisor, and a medical ethics committee could all see exactly how I used AI in this situation?" If any hesitation arises — that's information worth taking seriously.
2
Question Two
Is it allowed? Has my organization specifically authorized this tool for this type of use?
This is the compliance question. "Allowed" has a specific meaning in healthcare: your organization's IT and compliance teams have evaluated the tool, ensured it meets HIPAA requirements, and communicated authorization to staff. "I didn't see a rule against it" is not authorization. "A colleague uses it" is not authorization. "The vendor says it's secure" is not authorization.
✅ Clearly Allowed
Your organization has trained staff on the tool, it appears in your workflow documentation, and IT has approved it for patient data use
🚫 Not Allowed
A free AI tool you found online that seems helpful — even if it claims to be "HIPAA compliant" on its website without your organization verifying this
💡 When in doubt about authorization: ask your supervisor or IT department before using the tool with patient data. This takes 10 minutes and protects you completely. Acting without asking and getting it wrong can cost you your job or license.
3
Question Three
Is it safe? Have I reviewed the output critically and maintained my own professional judgment?
This is the oversight question — and it applies every time, even with trusted tools that have worked correctly before. AI outputs require human review. The safety question isn't just about the tool being secure — it's about whether you're maintaining your professional responsibility to verify what the AI produced before acting on it or distributing it.
✅ Safe Practice
You review the AI-generated clinical note against your own recollection of the visit, correct two inaccuracies, and sign off on the corrected version
🚫 Unsafe Practice
You skim the AI-generated note quickly and sign it because the AI "usually gets it right" and you're running behind — without actually verifying the content
💡 The safety check isn't just for documentation. If an AI risk score influences how you prioritize a patient, that output needs critical evaluation. If AI suggests a drug interaction check, verify it. The AI assists — you are responsible for what gets acted on.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Before any AI use: Is it appropriate (values test)? Is it allowed (authorization test)? Is it safe (oversight test)? All three must be yes. A "no" at any point means stop — not slow down, not find a workaround.

"Allowed" means your organization has specifically authorized the tool — not that you can't find a rule against it. When in doubt, ask before using with patient data.

The safety question applies every single time, even with familiar tools. AI outputs require human review before they are acted on or distributed — every time, not just when you have extra time.
