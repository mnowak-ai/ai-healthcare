Talking to Your Team About AI Recommendations: How to Explain, Question, and Build Consensus Around Data

How you communicate about AI recommendations shapes your team's entire relationship with AI tools. A manager who says "the AI told us to do this" creates very different team behavior than one who says "I reviewed the AI's recommendation and here is why I agreed." The words matter.

The Communication Gap Nobody Teaches

Every lesson in this module has focused on how individual healthcare workers receive and evaluate AI recommendations. But there is a second communication challenge equally important and almost never covered: how do you talk to your team about AI recommendations?

Whether you are a nurse manager explaining a staffing recommendation to your floor nurses, a scheduling coordinator asking a colleague about a patient situation that might affect an AI suggestion, or a practice manager introducing a new AI-assisted workflow — the way you communicate about AI decisions directly determines whether your team develops healthy critical engagement with these tools or passive compliance with them.

How you talk about AI recommendations sets the team's relationship with AI tools — and that relationship shapes whether the human oversight layer actually works.

Three Things Every AI Communication Should Do

1
Distinguish the recommendation from your decision
Always make clear: the AI flagged or recommended something, AND you reviewed it and agree — or modified it. Never present an AI recommendation as your decision without acknowledging your review. "The system says X" and "I reviewed the system's recommendation and decided X" create very different accountability cultures.
2
Share the reasoning in plain language
Explain the data behind the decision in operational terms, not technical ones. "The scheduling system predicted elevated volume based on patterns from the past three weeks" is useful. "The AI algorithm generated a recommendation" is not. People accept data-based decisions more readily when they understand the data.
3
Explicitly invite correction and pushback
Regularly and explicitly ask your team: "Does anyone have context I should know about before I act on this?" This signals that AI recommendations are inputs, not final answers — and that your team's knowledge is part of the decision process, not subordinate to the algorithm.

The Language That Builds vs. Erodes Critical Thinking

❌ Phrases That Erode Critical Thinking
"The AI says we need to..."
"The system decided..."
"We have to follow the recommendation..."
"The algorithm knows better than we do here..."
"Just do what the dashboard says..."
"I don't make these decisions — the AI does."
✅ Phrases That Build Critical Thinking
"The AI flagged this, and after reviewing it, I..."
"The data suggests X — does anyone see it differently?"
"I'm following the recommendation, but watch for..."
"What context might the AI be missing here?"
"The system is a starting point — your judgment matters more."
"I modified the recommendation because..."

Creating a feedback culture around AI recommendations — actively asking your team what context the system might be missing — is one of the most effective things a manager can do to keep the human oversight layer healthy.

📌 Key Takeaways

What to Remember From This Article

How you talk about AI recommendations sets your team's relationship with AI tools. "The AI told us to" creates passive compliance. "I reviewed the AI's recommendation and here is why I agreed" creates critical engagement. The words determine the culture.

Every AI communication should: distinguish the recommendation from your decision, share the reasoning in plain language, and explicitly invite pushback. All three together signal that AI is a tool your team evaluates — not an authority your team obeys.

Creating a team culture where people feel safe questioning AI recommendations is not optional. It is how the human oversight layer stays functional. Staff who feel they cannot push back on AI suggestions — because "the system decided" — will not catch the cases where the AI is wrong.
