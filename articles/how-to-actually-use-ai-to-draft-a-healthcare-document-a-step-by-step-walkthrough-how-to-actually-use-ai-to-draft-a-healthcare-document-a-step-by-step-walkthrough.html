Getting Started

Before You Write a Single Word

Using AI to draft healthcare documents can save you hours. But there's a catch. AI doesn't know your patient. It doesn't know your office policies. It doesn't know what happened last Tuesday.

That means you need to give it the right information. Think of AI as a very fast assistant who just started today. Smart, but clueless about the details. Your job is to bring them up to speed.

Pro Tip

Gather all your facts first. Patient name, dates, procedures, medications, insurance details. Having these ready makes everything faster.

Let's walk through the full process, step by step.

Step-by-Step

Writing Your First Prompt

A prompt is the instruction you give to an AI tool. It tells the AI what you want. Good prompts get good results. Bad prompts get confusing garbage.

1

State What You Need

Start by telling the AI exactly what kind of document you want. Be specific. "Write a letter" is too vague. "Write a prior authorization letter for a medication" is better.

2

Provide the Context

Give the AI the background info. Who is the patient? What is the diagnosis? What treatment are you requesting? Why is it needed? The AI can only work with what you give it.

3

Specify the Tone and Format

Do you need formal language? A bulleted list? A one-page summary? Tell the AI. It won't guess correctly on its own.

4

Include Constraints

Set limits. "Keep it under 300 words." "Use only information I provide." "Don't make up statistics." These rules keep the AI focused.

Example: A Good Prompt

Let's say you need to write a prior authorization letter for a patient who needs an MRI. Here's what a solid prompt looks like:

Sample Prompt Write a prior authorization letter for an MRI scan. Use these details:

• Patient: Sarah Chen, DOB 04/15/1985
• Insurance: BlueCross BlueShield, Member ID 12345678
• Diagnosis: Chronic lower back pain (ICD-10: M54.5)
• Requested procedure: Lumbar MRI without contrast
• Reason: Patient has not responded to 6 weeks of physical therapy and NSAIDs. Need to rule out disc herniation.
• Requesting provider: Dr. Maria Santos, MD

Keep the letter professional and under one page. Include all required information for insurance review.

See how specific that is? The AI now has everything it needs. No guessing required.

Fixing Problems

What to Do When the AI Draft is Wrong

Here's the truth: AI will make mistakes. Sometimes small ones. Sometimes big ones. Your job is to catch them all.

Hallucination is when AI makes up information that sounds real but isn't. It might invent a medication name. It might create a fake statistic. This is common, and it's dangerous in healthcare.

Common Problems to Watch For

!

Made-Up Facts

The AI might add details you never gave it. Maybe it says the patient "has been suffering for three years" when you never mentioned that. Delete anything you can't verify.

!

Wrong Medical Terms

AI might use outdated terms or mix up similar-sounding conditions. Always double-check medication names, diagnosis codes, and procedure names against official sources.

!

Incorrect Formatting

Insurance companies want things in a specific format. The AI might miss required fields like Tax ID numbers or NPI codes. Add these yourself.

!

Awkward Language

Sometimes AI writes sentences that are technically correct but sound weird. Read everything out loud. If it sounds strange, rewrite it.

Warning

Never trust AI-generated statistics, research citations, or claims about "studies show." These are often completely made up. If you need data, find it yourself from real sources.

How to Fix It: Follow-Up Prompts

You don't have to start over when something's wrong. You can ask the AI to fix specific problems. This is called iterating — making changes step by step.

Follow-Up Prompt Example Good start, but please make these changes:

• Remove the sentence about "studies showing 80% effectiveness" — I didn't provide that
• Add the provider's NPI number: 1234567890
• Make the closing paragraph more urgent — emphasize the patient is in significant pain

Keep fixing until the draft is right. Three or four rounds of edits is normal.

Quality Control

Your Pre-Send Verification Checklist

Before any healthcare document leaves your desk, run through this checklist. Every single time. No exceptions.

  • Patient Information: Is the name spelled correctly? Is the date of birth right? Is the member ID accurate?
  • Medical Accuracy: Are all diagnosis codes correct? Are medication names and dosages right? Are procedure codes accurate?
  • No Made-Up Content: Did you verify every fact in the document? Did you remove any statistics or claims you can't source?
  • Required Fields: Does the document include all fields the recipient needs? (NPI, Tax ID, dates of service, etc.)
  • Tone and Professionalism: Does it sound appropriate? Would your supervisor approve of this language?
  • Privacy Check: Are you sending this through a secure channel? Are you only including necessary information?
  • Final Read: Did you read the entire document out loud? Does everything make sense?
Remember

The AI wrote the draft. But you are responsible for what gets sent. If there's an error, it's your name on the line. Take verification seriously.

Privacy Matters

Protecting Patient Information

Here's something crucial: not all AI tools are safe for healthcare use. HIPAA is the law that protects patient health information. Breaking HIPAA rules can get you fired and fined.

Before using any AI tool with real patient data, ask yourself:

  • Is this tool approved by my workplace for healthcare use?
  • Does the company have a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with the AI provider?
  • Is my data encrypted and protected?

If you're not sure, use fake names and placeholder information while drafting. Then swap in the real details after. This keeps patient data safe while you work.

Never Do This

Never paste real patient names, Social Security numbers, or detailed medical histories into a free AI tool that hasn't been approved by your organization.

Key Terms

Glossary

Prompt

The instruction you give to an AI tool. It tells the AI what you want it to create.

Hallucination

When AI makes up information that sounds real but isn't true. Common with facts, statistics, and names.

Iterating

Making changes step by step. Asking the AI to fix specific problems in follow-up prompts.

Prior Authorization

A letter asking an insurance company to approve a treatment or procedure before it happens.

HIPAA

The law that protects patient health information. Breaking it can result in fines and job loss.

BAA

Business Associate Agreement. A legal contract that lets a company handle protected health data safely.

Real World Connection

How This Works in an Actual Job

Medical assistants, billing coordinators, and health administrators spend hours on paperwork. Prior authorizations. Appeals letters. Patient summaries. Insurance correspondence.

AI can cut that time dramatically. What used to take 45 minutes might take 10. But only if you know how to use it correctly.

Day in the Life

Maya is a medical assistant at a busy clinic. She gets three prior authorization requests before lunch. Using AI, she drafts all three letters in 20 minutes. She spends another 20 minutes verifying details and adding missing codes. By noon, all three are submitted. Without AI, that would have been her entire morning.

The skill isn't just "knowing AI exists." It's knowing how to prompt it, check it, and fix it. That's what makes you valuable.
