How to Evaluate a New AI Tool at Work: A Framework for Healthcare Professionals Who Aren't Technologists

When your employer introduces a new AI system, you don't need to be a software engineer to ask the right questions. This five-step framework gives you a clear, repeatable process for evaluating any AI tool in a healthcare context.

You Don't Need to Be Technical to Evaluate Technology

Healthcare workers often assume that evaluating AI tools is the job of IT or the CIO. It is not — not entirely. Frontline healthcare workers are the ones who will use these tools, catch their errors, and experience their impact on patient care. Your evaluation matters, and you have more relevant knowledge to contribute than you might think.

What you need is not technical knowledge. It is a structured set of questions that surface the information you need to use a tool responsibly and to flag concerns before they become problems.

The Five-Question Framework

🔍 AI Tool Evaluation Framework
Five Questions Before You Trust Any AI Tool in Healthcare
Apply these five questions every time your organization introduces a new AI tool. They are not technical questions — they are judgment questions.
1
What does this tool actually do — and what is it NOT designed to do?
Marketing materials describe what a tool does at its best. You need to understand its intended scope. An AI scheduling tool is not a clinical decision support tool. An AI documentation assistant is not a diagnostic tool. Knowing the boundaries helps you catch when the tool is being used outside its design.
2
Has it been validated in a setting like ours? Who did the testing?
AI tools tested in large academic medical centers may not perform the same way in a community clinic. Tools tested on one demographic may perform worse on others. And vendor-conducted testing has significant conflicts of interest. Ask whether independent, published validation exists — and whether the testing environment resembles your setting.
3
What are its failure modes — where and how does it get things wrong?
Every AI tool makes errors. The question is what type of errors and how often. A tool that occasionally gets dates wrong is different from one that consistently fails on older patients or non-English speakers. Systematic bias is the most dangerous failure mode. Ask specifically: "What types of errors has this tool made in real-world use?"
4
What are the privacy and compliance requirements for using it?
Is there a signed Business Associate Agreement? What data does the tool access and store? Has your organization's compliance team reviewed it? These questions are your responsibility as a healthcare professional, not just IT's. Using a tool without knowing the answers is a HIPAA risk that lands on you.
5
What does the human review layer look like — what am I expected to check?
No AI tool should be used without a defined human review step. Before using any AI tool, clarify: what outputs require human review before acting on them? What should you specifically look for when reviewing? Who is accountable when the tool produces an error that was acted on? If these questions don't have clear answers, that's a red flag.

What to Do When Something Seems Off

Using this framework will eventually surface concerns — errors you catch, patterns you notice, or compliance questions that nobody has answered. Here is what to do when that happens:

📋
Document it specifically
Write down what the tool did, what was wrong, and when it happened. Specific documentation turns a vague concern into actionable data that your organization can investigate.
🗣️
Report it through the right channel
Most healthcare organizations have a process for reporting technology concerns — IT help desk, compliance hotline, or supervisor escalation. Use the right channel, not a workaround, so the concern is documented and addressed systematically.
🔄
Do not fix it silently
If you catch an error, fix it — but also report it. Silently correcting AI errors without reporting them deprives your organization of the pattern data they need to improve or replace the tool.

The healthcare worker who asks good questions about AI tools is not a skeptic or an obstacle. They are the quality control layer that makes AI safe in clinical environments.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

You don't need to be technical to evaluate AI tools. You need five questions: What does it do (and not do)? Was it validated in a setting like yours? What are its failure modes? What are the privacy requirements? What is the human review layer?

When you notice a problem, document it specifically and report it through the right channel. Silently fixing errors deprives your organization of the pattern data needed to improve or replace the tool.
