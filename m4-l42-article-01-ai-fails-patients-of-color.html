Lesson 4.2 · AI Bias & Equity

When AI Fails Patients of Color: Real Cases of Bias in Healthcare AI

These aren't theoretical warnings. They're documented cases — published in major medical journals, investigated by hospitals, and now changing how the industry thinks about AI. Understanding them is the first step to making sure you never unknowingly contribute to them.

Why Real Cases Matter More Than Hypotheticals

It's easy to nod along when someone says "AI can be biased." It's a lot harder to ignore when you see exactly what that means for a real patient — the specific algorithm, the specific demographic, the specific harm that occurred because someone trusted a tool that wasn't built to serve them equally.

Every case in this article is real. The tools were deployed in actual hospitals. Real patients received worse care. Understanding the mechanism — not just the outcome — is what gives healthcare workers the ability to spot it happening and do something about it.

Four Documented Cases of Racial Bias in Healthcare AI

📊 Published in Science, 2019
The Hospital Risk Algorithm That Systematically Undertreated Black Patients
Optum algorithm · Used in hundreds of U.S. hospitals · Estimated 200M+ people affected annually
A widely-deployed algorithm used to identify patients who needed extra care — "high-risk care management programs" — was found to give Black patients significantly lower risk scores than white patients with the same level of illness. This meant Black patients were less likely to be enrolled in programs that could have significantly improved their health outcomes.
⚠️ The Flaw
The algorithm used healthcare spending as a proxy for how sick a patient was. Due to historical inequities, Black patients had spent less on healthcare even when equally ill — so the AI learned to associate lower spending with lower need, compounding existing inequality.
📉 The Real Impact
Researchers found that if the algorithm performed equally across races, the proportion of Black patients in high-risk care programs would have nearly doubled — from 17.7% to 46.5%. Thousands of Black patients were under-resourced for their actual health needs.
💡 What this means for you: When an AI tool uses a financial or behavioral proxy for a clinical concept, that proxy carries every historical inequality embedded in it. "The algorithm is just using data" is not a defense when that data reflects systemic inequity.
🩺 Published in NEJM, 2020 & 2022
Pulse Oximeters: When a Medical Device Was Never Tested on Darker Skin
FDA-approved devices · Widespread hospital use · COVID-19 exacerbated the harm
Pulse oximeters — the small clips that measure blood oxygen levels — are one of the most common medical devices in healthcare. During COVID-19, studies published in the New England Journal of Medicine revealed that pulse oximeters significantly overestimated oxygen saturation in patients with darker skin tones, showing readings that appeared normal when oxygen levels were dangerously low.
⚠️ The Flaw
The devices use light waves to measure oxygen. The algorithms were validated primarily on lighter-skinned patients. Melanin in darker skin interferes with the light in ways the calibration didn't account for — producing falsely reassuring readings for patients who needed intervention.
📉 The Real Impact
Black, Hispanic, and Asian patients were three times more likely to have "occult hypoxemia" — dangerously low oxygen levels that the device didn't detect. Some were denied supplemental oxygen, ICU care, or treatment based on falsely normal readings during COVID-19.
💡 What this means for you: Bias isn't limited to software algorithms. Any medical tool — including hardware — can embed bias through the populations it was tested on. The FDA has since updated guidance on pulse oximeter validation requirements, but millions of devices remain in use.
🧬 Documented in JAMA, 2020
Sepsis Prediction AI That Systematically Underestimated Severity in Black Patients
Epic Sepsis Model · Deployed across hospitals nationally
Sepsis — a life-threatening infection response — kills more than 270,000 Americans annually. AI-powered early warning tools are designed to detect it before it becomes fatal. A JAMA study auditing the Epic Sepsis Model found that the tool performed significantly worse for Black patients, who were substantially more likely to develop sepsis without receiving a high-risk alert.
⚠️ The Flaw
The model was trained on historical hospital data that already reflected disparities in care-seeking behavior, documentation practices, and clinical treatment. The patterns it learned weren't purely biological — they were shaped by the inequities already present in the healthcare system.
📉 The Real Impact
The model missed one in seven sepsis cases overall — but that miss rate was higher for Black patients. For a condition where hours of delay in treatment can mean death, being systematically under-flagged is a life-or-death disparity.
💡 What this means for you: An AI tool can be marketed as "clinically validated" while still performing unequally across demographic groups. "Validated" typically means "accurate on average" — which can still hide significant disparities within that average.
❤️ Documented in multiple cardiology studies
Cardiac Risk Tools Trained Overwhelmingly on White Male Patients
Framingham Risk Score and successors · Standard of care in cardiovascular medicine
The most widely used cardiac risk prediction tools in medicine were developed from long-running studies that enrolled predominantly white male participants. When applied to Black patients, women, and other groups underrepresented in the original studies, these tools systematically under- or over-estimate risk — sometimes with serious clinical consequences.
⚠️ The Flaw
The training populations didn't represent the diversity of real patients. The tools learned risk patterns from one demographic group and were then applied universally — without adequate validation studies for other populations.
📉 The Real Impact
Studies have shown that standard cardiac risk tools underestimate cardiovascular risk in Black patients, potentially leading to under-treatment. Women's heart attacks are also more frequently missed because women's cardiac events often present differently than the male-centric training data reflected.
💡 What this means for you: Some of these biased tools are still considered "standard of care." This is one of the most important reasons healthcare workers need to ask: "Was this tool validated for patients who look like the person in front of me right now?"

None of these tools were built to discriminate. No engineer set out to harm Black patients. But good intentions do not produce equitable outcomes when the data and validation processes don't account for population diversity. That's the core lesson of all four cases.

The question isn't whether the people who built these tools were racist. The question is whether the tools produce racist outcomes — and in every case above, they did. Intent and impact are different things, and in healthcare, impact is what harms or helps patients.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Racial bias in healthcare AI is not theoretical — it's documented in major medical journals across widely-deployed tools including hospital risk algorithms, pulse oximeters, sepsis predictors, and cardiac risk models.

The common mechanism: tools trained on data that underrepresents certain populations, or that uses proxy variables (like healthcare spending) that carry historical inequities, will systematically perform worse for those populations.

"FDA approved," "clinically validated," and "high overall accuracy" do not guarantee equitable performance across demographic groups. Every healthcare worker using AI tools has a professional responsibility to watch for — and report — patterns of unequal outcomes.
