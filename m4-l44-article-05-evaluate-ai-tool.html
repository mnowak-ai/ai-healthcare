How to Evaluate an AI Tool Before Using It in a Clinical or Administrative Setting

AI tools get introduced into healthcare settings in many ways — through organizational rollout, vendor demos, colleague recommendations, or department pilots. Before you use any new AI tool with patient data or clinical workflows, there's a structured evaluation you should run. This is that evaluation.

Why Evaluation Is a Professional Skill

Healthcare workers are not just consumers of AI tools — they're the humans responsible for how those tools are used. A tool that hasn't been properly evaluated is a tool whose failure modes you don't know, whose training data you haven't examined, and whose equity performance you can't defend.

Every question in the evaluation below has a corresponding risk if left unasked. Use this framework when a new AI tool is introduced to your department, when you're asked to participate in a pilot, or when you're in a role that influences which tools get adopted.

Red Flags That Should Pause Any Tool Adoption

Some answers to evaluation questions should immediately halt consideration of a tool — regardless of how good it looks otherwise:

🚩 No BAA available
Any vendor handling patient data who won't sign a Business Associate Agreement is automatically disqualified from HIPAA-regulated use. No exceptions.
🚩 Can't tell you training population
If the vendor can't describe who their tool was trained on and can't provide demographic performance breakdown, you cannot assess equity risk. This should disqualify the tool for clinical use.
🚩 "Validated" only on their own data
Internal vendor validation is not independent validation. A tool should have external validation studies, ideally peer-reviewed, on populations similar to yours.
🚩 No human review step in the workflow
Any tool designed to produce clinical outputs without a mandatory human review checkpoint before action is taken is a patient safety risk by design.
🚩 Accountability is unclear
If the vendor and your organization haven't explicitly discussed who is responsible when the tool is wrong — and that answer should be "the using institution and clinician" — the liability landscape is dangerously undefined.
🚩 No performance monitoring plan
A tool with no plan for ongoing performance review after deployment will drift without detection. Responsible integration includes monitoring, not just a one-time approval.
🧠 Quick Check
A vendor presents an AI diagnostic support tool with "98% accuracy." When asked about demographic performance breakdown, they say the tool is "accurate across all patient types" but can't provide data by race, sex, or age group. What should happen next?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Evaluating AI tools is a professional skill — not just an IT function. Healthcare workers who participate in tool adoption decisions, department pilots, or workflow design have a responsibility to ask the questions that protect patients.

Six automatic red flags: no BAA, unknown training population, vendor-only validation, no human review step, undefined accountability, and no post-deployment monitoring plan. Any of these should pause adoption until resolved.

Demographic performance data is not optional. "Accurate across all patient types" without supporting data is a marketing claim. Before a tool with clinical applications is deployed, equity performance must be documented and defensible.
