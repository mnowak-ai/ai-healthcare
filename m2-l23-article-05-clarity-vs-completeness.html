Lesson 2.3 · Editing AI Outputs

Clarity vs. Completeness: How to Edit AI Documents That Sound Clear But Are Missing Key Information

AI is very good at making documents sound clear and well-organized. It is much less good at making sure everything that needs to be there actually is. This article teaches you the subtle but critical skill of checking not just whether a document reads well — but whether it contains everything it must.

The Clarity Trap

When you read an AI-generated document and think "this reads well," your brain often stops there. The writing flows. The sentences make sense. The tone feels right. But there's a completely separate question that clarity doesn't answer: Is everything that needs to be here actually here?

AI produces documents that are clear and well-structured — but it can only include information that was in your prompt. If you didn't mention a follow-up date, it won't be there. If you didn't specify who is responsible for an action item, the AI may write "the team will follow up" and move on. A document can read beautifully and still be missing the information that makes it useful.

A document that reads well and is missing critical information is more dangerous than one that reads poorly. The smooth writing lulls you into thinking it's complete.

Clear vs. Complete: The Same Information, Both Ways

Here's the same clinical follow-up email written in two ways — one that's clear and easy to read, one that's both clear AND complete. Notice the difference:

⚠️ Clear But Incomplete
Subject: Your Follow-Up Information Dear Mr. Patterson, We wanted to follow up after your recent visit. Your doctor has reviewed your test results and would like to discuss the next steps with you. Please call our office to schedule your follow-up appointment at your earliest convenience. Thank you, Green Valley Clinic
❌ Reads smoothly — but: Which test results? What next steps? Which doctor? What phone number? When is "earliest convenience" — this week, this month? The patient has no actionable information.
✅ Clear AND Complete
Subject: Follow-Up — Your June 12 Lab Results Dear Mr. Patterson, Dr. Reyes has reviewed your June 12th lab results and would like to go over the findings with you in person. This is routine — nothing urgent. Please call us at (555) 204-1100 (Mon–Fri, 8am–5pm) to schedule. We have appointments available this week. Thank you, Green Valley Clinic
✅ Clear AND complete: specific doctor, specific test date, reassuring context, specific phone number, specific hours, specific timeframe. The patient can act on this immediately.

Five Things AI Commonly Leaves Out

These are the elements AI most frequently omits because they weren't in the prompt — or because the AI smoothed over the gap with vague language:

🔍 What AI Leaves Out
Five Common Completeness Gaps
Each of these sounds fine when you read over it quickly — that's what makes them dangerous.
👤

Named responsible person → replaced by "the team" or "staff"

AI avoids specifying who is responsible for an action because it doesn't know. "The team will follow up" is grammatically fine but creates zero accountability.

❌ "The care team will arrange the follow-up appointment."
✅ "J. Rivera, MA will call Mr. Thompson by June 17 to schedule the follow-up."
📅

Specific deadline → replaced by "soon," "promptly," or "as soon as possible"

AI uses vague time language because it doesn't know what "soon" means in your context. "Schedule promptly" means nothing to a patient going home at 5pm on a Friday.

❌ "Please follow up with your primary care physician soon."
✅ "Please follow up with your primary care physician within 7 days — by June 22nd."
📞

Contact information → replaced by "contact our office" or "reach out to your provider"

AI can't know your phone number, extension, email, or hours. "Contact us" with no information is an instruction the patient can't follow.

❌ "If you have questions, please contact our office."
✅ "If you have questions, call us at (555) 204-1100, Mon–Fri 8am–5pm."
⚠️

Warning signs or escalation criteria → entirely absent

Discharge letters and follow-up communications should tell patients when to seek immediate care. AI frequently omits these red flag criteria entirely unless specifically prompted.

❌ "Please continue to monitor your recovery at home."
✅ "Contact us immediately or go to the ER if you develop fever above 101°F, increased pain, redness at the incision site, or difficulty breathing."
🔢

Specific doses, quantities, or measurements → replaced by vague references

AI sometimes references a medication or instruction without the specific quantity, because it doesn't know the prescribed amount.

❌ "Take your pain medication as prescribed."
✅ "Take ibuprofen 400mg by mouth every 6 hours as needed for pain. Maximum 3 doses per day."

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

A clear document is not necessarily a complete document. AI is skilled at producing smooth, readable text — but it only includes what was in your prompt. Completeness is a separate check that asks: "Does the reader have everything they need to act on this?"

Five things AI most commonly leaves out: named responsible person (replaced by "the team"), specific deadline (replaced by "soon"), specific contact information, warning signs/escalation criteria, and specific doses or quantities.

The completeness test: after reading an AI draft, ask yourself "Could the reader act on every instruction in here without calling to ask for more information?" If any instruction requires a follow-up question to be actionable, it's incomplete.
