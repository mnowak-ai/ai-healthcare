What Is Protected Health Information (PHI)? The Complete Guide With Real Examples

PHI is the core of HIPAA protection. But most people have a much narrower idea of what counts as PHI than the law actually requires. Knowing the full picture — especially how it applies to AI — is essential before you start any healthcare role.

PHI Isn't Just Medical Records

When most people think of "protected health information," they picture a patient's medical chart. But PHI is a much broader category than that. Under HIPAA, PHI is defined as any information that could identify a specific person AND relates to their health, healthcare, or payment for healthcare.

That definition has two parts — and both parts matter. Health data alone isn't necessarily PHI. A name alone isn't PHI. But when they're linked — even indirectly — they become PHI, and HIPAA applies.

This distinction becomes critically important with AI tools, because AI can often link data that humans wouldn't think to connect. An AI that knows someone's zip code, age, gender, and employer — even without a name — may be able to identify specific individuals. This is part of why healthcare privacy in the AI era is more complex than it used to be.

What PHI Is Made Of: The Two-Part Formula

PHI exists when BOTH of these are true:

🏥
Health Information
Diagnosis, treatment, test results, medications, mental health, payment history

Context Changes Everything: When the Same Data Is or Isn't PHI

One of the trickiest things about PHI is that whether something counts as PHI can depend entirely on context. The same piece of data can be PHI in one situation and not PHI in another. This is especially important when using AI tools that process data at scale:

🔄 Context Matters
PHI or Not PHI? It Depends on Context
The same data point can cross the line depending on what it's paired with.
The Data
Context A
Context B
A person's age (45)
✅ NOT PHI — "45-year-old patients in our study had better outcomes" (population statistic, no individual linked)
🔒 PHI — "Patient John Smith, age 45, was treated for…" (age linked to name and diagnosis)
Age alone is not PHI. Age combined with a name and health information is PHI — HIPAA fully applies.
A zip code
✅ NOT PHI — "78% of patients in zip code 90210 completed their follow-ups" (no individual identified)
🔒 PHI — The last 3 digits of a zip code paired with date of birth and gender can uniquely identify up to 87% of people in the U.S.
This is the famous "87% re-identification" finding. Even limited demographic data can identify specific individuals — which is why AI de-identification is more complex than it appears.
A photo
✅ NOT PHI — A stock photo of a hospital waiting room with no identifiable patients
🔒 PHI — A photo of a patient in a clinical setting, even without their name, because the image itself identifies them
Full-face photos are one of the 18 PHI identifiers. This matters for AI tools that process or analyze medical imaging or clinical photos.
A device serial number
✅ NOT PHI — The serial number of a general-use hospital blood pressure cuff
🔒 PHI — The serial number of an implanted pacemaker or CPAP machine assigned to a specific patient
Device identifiers are one of the 18 PHI identifiers when the device is personal health equipment assigned to an identifiable patient. This matters for wearables and connected health devices.

PHI and AI: What You Can and Can't Do

AI tools in healthcare often process data at a scale and speed that makes PHI risks harder to see. Here's a clear breakdown of what creates PHI risk with AI — and what doesn't:

🚫 High PHI Risk — Avoid Without Compliance Review
Entering Any of These Into Non-Approved AI Tools
⛔ Patient name + any health info
⛔ Medical record numbers, even partial
⛔ "The patient born 3/15/1978 with diabetes who was seen Monday…"
⛔ Photos or images from clinical settings with patients visible
⛔ Any combination of demographics + diagnosis
⛔ Copying text from a patient's chart into an AI prompt
✅ Lower PHI Risk — Generally Acceptable
Using AI Tools for These Purposes
✔ Drafting general communication templates with no patient info
✔ Learning about medical conditions in general terms ("how is Type 2 diabetes managed?")
✔ Creating staff-facing educational content with no patient data
✔ Using employer-approved, HIPAA-compliant AI tools with patient data
✔ Analyzing completely de-identified datasets that meet HIPAA's Safe Harbor standard

When in doubt, ask: "Could this information be used to identify a specific patient?" If yes — it's PHI. Treat it accordingly.

🧠 Quick Check
A healthcare worker wants to use an AI tool to analyze patient no-show patterns. The data they plan to use includes: appointment dates, patient zip codes, ages, and insurance type — but NO names or medical record numbers. Is this PHI?
📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

PHI requires two things together: health information AND an identifier that could link it to a specific person. Remove either part and it may no longer be PHI. Keep both and HIPAA fully applies.

HIPAA recognizes 18 official identifiers — including things most people don't expect, like zip codes, device serial numbers, photos, IP addresses, and even the description of rare conditions that can identify someone indirectly.

Context changes everything. The same data can be PHI or not PHI depending on what it's paired with. AI makes this harder because AI tools can re-identify data that seems anonymous by combining seemingly harmless fields.

The working test: before entering any information into an AI tool, ask "Could this be used to identify a specific patient?" If yes — treat it as PHI, use only approved tools, and when uncertain, ask your supervisor.
