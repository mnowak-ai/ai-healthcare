Lesson 1.4 · Roles Impacted by AI in Healthcare

Real-World Spotlight: How Mount Sinai Health System Uses AI Across Clinical and Administrative Roles

Mount Sinai is one of the most AI-active health systems in the country — and uniquely, their AI investments touch every type of healthcare role simultaneously. This deep dive shows what AI-integrated work looks like for administrators, clinicians, and technical staff within the same organization.

Why Mount Sinai Stands Out

Most health system AI case studies focus on one department or one type of tool. Mount Sinai's AI journey is distinctive because they've pursued AI integration across the full spectrum of healthcare roles simultaneously — administrative, clinical, and technical — with a dedicated research arm (the Hasso Plattner Institute for Digital Health at Mount Sinai) publishing transparent findings about both successes and challenges.

That transparency makes them an exceptional learning case. You can see not just where AI is working at Mount Sinai, but where they've hit walls, had to course-correct, and learned what implementation actually requires.

How AI Touches Different Roles at Mount Sinai

What makes Mount Sinai's approach particularly valuable for this certification is that their AI programs affect every type of healthcare role differently. Here's how the same organization's AI investments land for three different staff categories:

💼
Administrative Staff
AI scheduling tools reduce manual booking time for complex multi-specialty cases
Automated insurance verification frees staff for patient-facing work
Patient chatbot handles routine scheduling queries before they reach human staff
Human role: exception handling, distressed patient support, override decisions
🩺
Clinical Staff
Sepsis and deterioration AI alerts fire in the EHR — nurses assess and respond
Ambient documentation AI drafts notes — providers review and sign
AI imaging tools flag potential abnormalities — radiologists make final reads
Human role: all final clinical decisions, patient communication, ethical judgment
🖥️
Health IT Staff
Monitor AI tool performance across the system — track accuracy over time
Manage EHR integrations for third-party AI tools
Train clinical staff on new AI features and responsible use
Human role: AI governance, performance monitoring, staff education, vendor relations

Mount Sinai's approach shows that AI in healthcare isn't a clinical technology or an administrative technology — it's both simultaneously, and every role in the organization experiences it differently and carries different responsibilities around it.

📌 Article Summary

What to Remember

Mount Sinai deploys AI across administrative, clinical, and technical functions simultaneously — and each role category experiences AI differently, with different oversight responsibilities and different types of human judgment required.

Mount Sinai's transparency about AI challenges — including alert fatigue, implementation difficulties, and performance limitations — is as valuable as their successes. Responsible AI adoption includes honest assessment of what isn't working.

AI governance — the ongoing monitoring, feedback collection, and recalibration of AI tools — is itself a healthcare job that requires humans. AI deployment is never "set and forget"; it requires continuous human oversight at the organizational level.
